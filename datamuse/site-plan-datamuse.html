Home page

We've consolidated the entire day's sales at a couple grocery stores into two minutes. Each transaction within that 5-minute window gets grouped together.
Tone is building on this melody: CGEGEDC
Instrumentation depends on the type of purchase that customer made:
Gas purchases are a low rumble, like an engine passing by
Non-alcoholic drinks are flutes
Alcoholic drinks are bassoon
Vapes & cigarettes are trombones
Snacks are the piano
Lottery tickets are the glockenspiel
Other items (cashing checks, electronics) are a harp
Volume comes from the size of the purchase. We standardized each category, as snacks purchases are consistently smaller than, say, filling a gas tank.

About Data Sonification

What is Data Sonification?
In data visualizations, we turn a database of entries into a picture, tying specific heights, colors, or shapes to attributes and columns of the data (big numbers become tall bars, etc.). In data sonification, we can tie those same columns to aspects of music, such as volume, tempo, pitch, and instrumentation.
Why Data Sonification?
Data visualizations, infographics, and comprehensive charts are popular right now because they express complicated ideas in simple, engaging ways. However, our bodies collect and process information in so many ways, not just through eyes. Many species around the world rely on scent, hearing and touch to navigate spaces, and we live in a digital world that uses almost exclusively vision.
How Does it Work?
We’ve used the musicpy package in Python, which can match text strings of beats and note names into sound files. As we grab column, we can rewrite them in the syntax of musicpy then mix the results all together into a single music arrangement.
